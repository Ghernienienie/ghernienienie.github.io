CCA

I first joined the Basketball CCA for 1 year in Primary 2. Despite the thrill and excitement for each and every dribble of the ball, I came to a conclusion that competitive sport was not my forte.

I preferred somewhere where I can be more expressive, where I can feel heightened senses while still being relaxed. Having that in mind, I explored and waited to stumble upon that oasis.

On one dim and breezy afternoon, my ears captured a sound - so loud, yet so serene. While the musical bridge of sound waves were still strong, I tracked the source of the sound; and when I had discovered the source, I observed a man, in tears, grasping his rod-like instrument tightly, as though he was hugging it and expressing his emotions in it. My inspiration bloomed.

Eagerly, I rushed towards the musical co-curricular activities' venues in my school and broke an entry into a live room that smelled of pungent brass. A man with a baton and his band of young musicians halt, staring at me with shock. With shame, I apologized. As I turned a hundred and eighty degrees from their direction, a resonating "wait" from the man lingered in the room. At that moment, I had accepted my fate - I am doomed. "Would you like to try to play an instrument?", he announced. From then on, my life changed forever.

Though I started off with the band at later time than the majority of the players, I was able to catch up surprisingly swift. I always looked forward to the practices that we had. I held my horn in e-flat from the moment I joined the brass band to the very graduation day where my last rhapsody took off.

CCA

Initially, I wanted to switch path from the musical world to the digital world; I first wanted to join the school's Infocomm Club - which is a co-curricular activity involving photography, video-graphy and digital editing - which I had passion for.

On the CCA's try out day held in my school, the queue for audition to join the club stretched from the interviewer all the way across the school's hall, which is jaw-droppingly long, that queue was the barrier. Had I waited, time for my audition would be insufficient.

I decided to rejoin a band. This time, it was Symphonic Band. Disappointment never came to my attention since. My new partner was the Trombone, and I would carry and perform with it in our school's as well as external events.

It was only until Secondary 3 when I had to trade my Trombone for the Trumpet due to the band's lack of players. It was mainly because of a due performance coming in a month's time that made me volunteered to play it. My consistency and adaptability enabled me to play the Trumpet with a pleasant tone during the Singapore's Youth Band Festival of 2019.

It was always obvious that my peers, seniors and juniors admired my commitment to the band. I loved being the best example that I could be to them, and more. As a result, I was often elected the proclaimed busiest roles in the band.

I was first appointed as a Quarter Master in Secondary 2 to 3, which is a manager for the logistics matter of the band, and this mainly includes ordering of instrumental accessories and checking of instruments to send for maintenance. I had introduced many novelties in planning such as starting an excel sheet for the logistics instead of sticking to the traditional black and white of a pen.

My responsibility in that role lead me to being the Drum Major of the band in Secondary 3 to 4, which was proclaimed the role of a disciplinarian and administrator in my band.

Highlights of me in this role includes similarities as when I was in the QM role; I also introduced admired novelties in the band and established clean and standardized information regarding every administrative matter of the band including the specific duties of every role holder in the band, as well as instructions on what and how to execute their duties.

I left a legacy of being: an extremely committed individual of the band; being the first student to conduct a National Day Parade (whom previously were teachers); being the student who learned the trumpet within a month, and finally having established structured rules and procedures for the band.
